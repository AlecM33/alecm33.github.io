MIDSOMMAR

It’s certainly not safe, lightly-salted horror. It’s probably not the horror you’d pick as the capstone to a nice, romantic outing with a significant other. Sometimes, it’s simply complete lunacy. But it’s horror. And it’s got a beautiful, unforgettable personality.

- - -

I left the theater after seeing Midsommar in a haze. For me, one mark of a unique film is my difficulty in quickly and decisively analyzing it. How did I feel about it? What was it trying to tell me? This film doesn’t yield easy answers to these questions. Did I like it? Yes. That one’s easy. I felt fear, shock, sadness, discomfort – I even felt the urge to laugh uncontrollably. All these emotions packed into one dizzyingly psychedelic picture result in nothing other than an enjoyable ride.

Ari Aster is solidifying himself as one of the most intriguing horror directors today. He has a good knowledge of what it takes for something to be truly horrifying. Not cheap jump scares tied to a predictable symphonic blast, and not yet another slow, blindly curious walk through a haunted house (I’m looking at you, James Wan). What makes something horrifying is its ability to truly subvert expectations, and take the mind to a place it fully did not expect--or want--to go. Midsommar reminded me heavily of Guadagnino’s Suspiria, in that it relies heavily on beautifully novel and often terrifying imagery to evoke fear and discomfort. There’s a complete disregard for what should and shouldn’t be shown on the screen. It’s complete artistic free-roaming.

The place that Midsommar roams to is gorgeous. It’s a commune in the Swedish countryside, and it’s a setting for a horror film unlike any other. The film happens almost entirely in the daytime. The frame is dominated by simple yet intricately decorated architecture, and motifs of pure white and flowers. It’s a bold and welcome juxtaposition. The set and costume design is beautifully colorful and baroque. The shots are often symmetrical and encompassing, with slow sweeping pans balanced with jarring cuts. There isn’t a lot of obvious CGI, save for drug-induced psychedelia. On a technical level, the film is a marvel to look at.

Family struck me as a central theme. In this way, and in other ways, the film parallels Hereditary. It explores what family means beyond a simple blood relation. Empathy, support through loss, unconditional love – these are things which we believe constitute true care. When the movie’s central relationship goes sour, we see that while it was strong and serene on the surface, the foundation wasn’t what it seemed – much like the seemingly pure and idyllic setting. Further, if a sense of community grows too strong, and ancestral tradition entrenches itself to the point of unquestionable loyalty, we see the maddening and grotesque effects this can have on us all.

That said, I’m not madly in love with the film. There’s so much to like about it – the set design, the imagery, the face-melting score – but there are things which I believe take away from it. Chief among these is the absolute derailment of the third act. I was confronted with nudity, gore, and nonsensical derangement that gave my friends and I no choice but to laugh in disbelief. Like I said previously, laughter in a horror film doesn’t dry up my enjoyment, but it certainly doesn’t seem right for me to be laughing. Though there are jokes, the film is by no means a comedy. By my account, the scenes which evoked laughter were certainly meant to be taken seriously. This blunted the razor’s edge that I believe Ari Aster wants his films to have. These scenes may very well serve his purpose in depicting true madness, but to me they are just needless excess. To their credit, they don’t feel so out of place in the setting that the movie establishes.

In the end, I emphasize that this is a unique voice in horror. It’s not easy viewing, and that’s how the best of the genre should be. So, in that regard, I fully endorse seeing it. It’s certainly not safe, lightly-salted horror. It’s probably not the horror you’d pick as the capstone to a nice, romantic outing with a significant other. Sometimes, it’s simply complete lunacy. But it’s horror. And it’s got a beautiful, unforgettable personality.
